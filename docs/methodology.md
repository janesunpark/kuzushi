# Methodology

## Overview

Kuzushi uses a longitudinal, practitioner-observation approach to document learning behaviors across academic, spatial, game-based, and embodied contexts. The project is exploratory and naturalistic rather than experimental. Its purpose is to track patterns in how young learners respond to challenge, select strategies, recover from difficulty, and adapt across tasks over time. 

The methodology centers on a multi-layered synthesis process. Private observations are collected after sessions, compressed into private weekly synthesis logs, and then selectively distilled into anonymized public-facing research notes. This process is designed to preserve learner privacy while making broader learning patterns available for documentation, portfolio review, and future exploratory analysis.

## Observation Context

Kuzushi is based primarily on small-group academic enrichment sessions involving early math, spatial puzzles, strategic games, worksheet tasks, play-based exploration, and co-regulated learning. Periodic observations from youth Jiu Jitsu contexts serve as a secondary exploratory thread when relevant to problem-solving, strategy selection, regulation, or adaptation under constraint. 

The observation context is naturalistic. Sessions are shaped by learner readiness, task difficulty, motivation, regulation needs, instructional supports, and the relationship dynamics of repeated learning interactions. Because the project follows a small number of learners over time, the methodology emphasizes pattern tracking, cautious interpretation, and longitudinal synthesis rather than generalization. 

## Data Sources

Current project materials include:

* private Session Observation Logs
* private Weekly Synthesis Logs
* selected public Weekly Research Notes
* academic year-end summaries
* periodic embodied observation notes

Raw observation logs, exact dates, locations, schedules, photos, videos, and sensitive child-specific details are not published. 

## Observation and Synthesis Process

Private Session Observation Logs are created after academic enrichment sessions. These logs document session context, task materials, learner response, scaffolding, motivation, co-regulation, task persistence, problem-solving behavior, and observable strategy use.

Some observational categories are tracked using practitioner-generated numerical ratings for future exploratory visualization and analysis. These ratings are not formal assessments or validated measures. They are used as internal pattern-tracking tools to help organize observations over time. 

Session observations are then generally paired and compressed into private Weekly Synthesis Logs. These synthesis logs identify recurring patterns, notable shifts, possible learning mechanisms, and data flags for future monitoring. They function as an intermediate layer between raw private observations and public-facing project documentation. 

Selected public Weekly Research Notes are then distilled from private logs and synthesis notes. These notes are anonymized, pattern-level, and organized around recurring themes such as cognitive stamina, co-regulation, motivation, reward systems, spatial reasoning, strategy adaptation, and possible cross-context problem-solving patterns. 

## Public-Facing Research Notes

Public-facing research notes are not comprehensive session records. They are selected summaries written to document broader learning patterns while minimizing identifiability. They do not include raw data, full transcripts, private family or school context, exact schedules, media, or sensitive details. 

Weekly Research Notes are treated as exploratory artifacts. They help preserve the project's longitudinal reasoning process and create a foundation for future derived data schemas, codebooks, exploratory notebooks, and visualizations. 

## Interpretation Boundaries

Kuzushi uses cautious interpretive language. Public-facing notes rely on terms such as "appeared to," "suggested," "may," and "exploratory" to distinguish observation from conclusion. The project does not make causal claims, diagnostic claims, or generalizable claims about child development. 

Observed patterns are treated as signals for future observation rather than formal findings. Cross-context observations involving Jiu Jitsu or embodied learning are handled especially cautiously and are not treated as evidence of transfer or causation. 

## Relationship to Data Ethics

Privacy and anonymization are central to the methodology. Raw observation logs and sensitive private data remain undisclosed. Learners are referenced only through anonymized labels, and public-facing examples are edited to reduce identifiability while preserving the relevant learning pattern. 

Detailed privacy and anonymization practices are described in [Data Ethics & Anonymization](data_ethics.md).

## Future Structured Data Work

Future work may convert derived observations into structured data schemas or codebooks for exploratory analysis. Potential categories may include:

* task type
* cognitive domain
* learner response
* support type
* regulation event
* strategy observed
* task constraint level
* motivation or reward context
* cross-context observation flag
* data flags for future monitoring 

These derived structures will be used for exploratory analysis only. They are not intended for learner evaluation, prediction, diagnosis, or ranking. 

## Use of AI Assistance 

AI-assisted tools were used to support organization, drafting, and editing of public-facing documentation. The author remained responsible for observation, interpretation, anonymization, and final review.
