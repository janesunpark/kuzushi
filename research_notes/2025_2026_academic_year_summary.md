# 2025—2026 Academic Year Summary

## Overview

During the 2025–2026 academic year, Kuzushi developed as a longitudinal observation project focused on early math learning, spatial reasoning, motivation, emotional regulation, and problem-solving behavior in a small-group academic enrichment setting. 

In the fall semester, the class format prioritized emotional safety, engagement, and positive association with learning. A significant portion of each session was reserved for self-directed exploration using STEM games and play-based materials. This structure helped support the learners' transition into a more formal learning environment while allowing observation of early patterns in autonomy, curiosity, frustration recovery, and collaborative engagement. During this period, learners showed growth in participation, focus, confidence, and willingness to engage with structured tasks. 

In the spring semester, the class format became more structured in response to increased cognitive stamina and task readiness. Free play time was gradually reduced, worksheet expectations increased, and a multi-layered reward system was introduced to support motivation, visible progress, and sustained effort. Both learners demonstrated increased cognitive output over time, including greater worksheet completion, stronger number sense, improved emotional regulation, and increaed willingness to attempt more challenging tasks. 

Across the year, spatial puzzles and worksheet tasks became especially useful for observing problem-solving behavior. Learners showed emerging patterns in strategy selection, pattern recognition, shape manipulation, cognitive flexibility, and recovery after difficulty. While learner responses to structure, rewards, and challenge varied, both learners appeared to develop more stable routines for engaging with academic and play-based tasks. 

By the end of the 2025—2026 cycle, Kuzushi had begun to identify several exploratory pattern categories, including cognitive stamina, co-regulation, motivation, spatial reasoning, strategy adaptation, and possible cross-context problem-solving patterns. These observations are treated as preliminary signals rather than formal findings and will guide future public research notes, derived data structures, and exploratory analysis. 

## Project Scope

This summary covers the 2025–2026 academic year observation cycle of Kuzushi. The project followed a small number of anonymized young learners in a small-group academic enrichment setting, with primary attention to early math learning, spatial reasoning, frustration recovery. motivation, co-regulation, and problem-solving behavior.

The scope of this document is limited to broad learning patterns observed across the year. It does not include raw session logs, identifying details, exact dates, videos, photos, or sensitive child-specific information. Specific examples are included only when they can be described at a pattern level and without increasing identifiability. 

Embodied movement observations, specifically youth Jiu Jitsu classes, are treated as a secondary exploratory thread. They are included only when relevant to possible cross-context problem-solving patterns and are not used to claim transfer, causation, or performance outcomes. 

This summary should be read as an exploratory practitioner-observation artifact rather than a formal research study, clinical assessment, curriculum evaluation, or generalizable claim about child development. 

## Observation Context

Observations collected during the 2025–2026 cycle consisted of small-group academic enrichment sessions involving early math, spatial puzzles, strategic games, play-based exploration, and co-regulated learning. Class structure in the fall semester included more self-directed exploration and free play, while the spring semester became more structured, with increased worksheet tasks, spatial puzzles, and reward-cycle routines.

The learners were young children from English language learner (ELL) backgrounds and were new to a structured learning environment. In both private and public-facing materials, they referred to only through anonymized labels. The learners also regularly participated in youth Jiu Jitsu classes. Observations made in these embodied learning contexts were periodic and secondary, used only when relevant to problem-solving, strategy selection, regulation, or adaptation under constraint. 

Exact dates, locations, schedules, and identifying details are intentionally omitted to protect the learners' privacy and follow ethical research practices.
