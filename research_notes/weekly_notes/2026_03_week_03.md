# Weekly Research Note: Co-Regulatory Support, Comparison Dynamics, and Varied Responses to a Multi-Layered Reward System 

## Snapshot

Across this week, both learners showed brief disengagement followed by relatively quick recovery with co-regulatory support from a trusted adult. Despite limited environmental novelty, both remained engaged with structured tasks and showed different responses to the multi-layered reward system. These patterns suggested emerging self-directed reward management, comparison-based motivation, and growing ownership of session pace. 

## Key Observations

Both learners experienced moments of brief disengagement during the week but were able to return to task completion after co-regulatory support. Teacher support appeared to help reestablish session flow by redirecting attention toward each learner's own progress, task goals, and available next steps. 

S01 and S02 both completed additional worksheet tasks to earn sticker rewards, but their responses to the reward system appeared to differ. S01 showed strong enthusiasm for earning stickers and completing the reward image, suggesting that visible progress markers supported her engagement. S02 appeared more sensitive to reward novelty, reward value, and comparison dynamics. His engagement shifted depending on whether the available reward, competitive play, or comparison with S01 appeared more motivating in the moment. 

## Learning Mechanisms

This week suggested that the reward system functioned as more than a simple incentive. For S01, the sticker-based reward structure appeared to support task completion, visible progress, and pride in finishing a cycle. For S02, the same structure appeared more varied: in one session, he opted out of additional worksheet tasks in favor of competitive game time, while in a later session he completed additional pages after finishing the reward cycle in order to earn stickers toward the next cycle. 

These differences may reflect emerging learner-specific patterns in reward management. S01 appeared motivated by visible completion, progress toward a concrete reward image, and comparison dynamics. S02 appeared to weigh reward novelty, reward value, competitive play, and comparison dynamics when deciding whether to continue worksheet tasks or shift into play. Both learners showed signs of using the reward system actively rather than responding to it passively. 

Co-regulatory support also appeared to play an important role in sustaining engagement. When comparison dynamics or brief disengagement disrupted session flow, calm redirection helped shift attention back toward individual progress and task completion. This suggests that reward systems may be most effective when paired with adult support that helps learners interpret progress without becoming overly focused on comparison. 

## Emerging Questions

* How do reward novelty and environmental novelty affect cognitive stamina and task output?
* How do learners use reward systems to manage task engagement, pacing, and re-engagement? 
* When does comparison-based motivation support persistence, and when does it require co-regulatory support?
