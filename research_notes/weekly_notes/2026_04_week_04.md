# Weekly Research Note: Novelty, Reward Value, and Strategy Adoption in Spatial Problem-Solving

## Snapshot

This week's sessions revealed variable learner responses to novelty in different contexts, including task challenge, reward conditions, task format, and spatial problem-solving approach. Recurring behavioral patterns around co-regulatory support, reward novelty, and perceived reward value reinforced prior observations about learner-specific learning styles and future exploratory threads. 

## Key Observations

Across the week, both learners showed variable responses to novelty across different contexts.
In one session, S02 maintained a relatively stable output under conditions of low reward novelty and reward value while continuing to seek novelty in the tasks themselves. For instance, he translated brief illustrated stories into addition and subtraction number sentences with teacher support for the first time, making an early scaffold toward word-problem reasoning. 

In another session, S02 responded positively to increased reward novelty and reward value, which appeared to support learner engagement and performance across multiple domains. This suggested both task novelty and reward novelty may have contributed to session engagement. 

Both learners had been recently introduced to a new problem-solving approach for a challenging spatial task: decomposing the pattern into smaller component parts and working methodically unit by unit. During this week's version of the same activity, however, both S01 and S02 matched familiar visual patterns first rather than following the teacher-demonstrated decomposition entry point. 

This week's sessions also included a less constrained spatial puzzle that allowed for collaboration. Under the same conditions, S01 approached the task through close-fitting, visually organized placement. In contrast, S02 used a more improvisational strategy, relocating pieces as needed rather than organizing tightly from the start. During the activity, the teacher joined the collaborative effort and modeled how to identify pieces that fit empty spaces precisely. S01 immediately adopted the same strategy, suggesting strong responsiveness to teacher modeling alongside visual closure and spatial fit. 

## Learning Mechanisms

This week suggested that novelty may function differently depending on whether it appears in the reward system, the task itself, or the broader learning environment. S02's stable output under low reward novelty, combined with his interest in novel task formats, suggests that task novelty may help sustain engagement even when reward novelty is limited. His stronger engagement under higher reward novelty and value suggests that reward conditions may still influence motivation and output, although not in isolation. 

The spatial puzzle observations reinfored a recurring pattern in both learners' problem-solving behavior under greater constraint. When faced with a challenging spatial task, both learners appeared to use familiar visual patterns as entry points, even after being shown a more methodical decomposition strategy. This may suggest that familiar-pattern-first approaches support task initiation, but future observations should examine whether they also limit flexibility when a different strategy is more efficient. 

The less constrained collaborative puzzle showed clearer learner-specific differences. S01's close-fitting, visually organized approach suggested strong visual-structural organization and sensitivity to spatial fit. Her immediate adoption of the teacher's modeled strategy also suggested responsiveness to visual demonstration and guided refinement. S02's more improvisational approach suggested a greater willingness to explore through trial, adjustment, and relocation when task constraints were lower. Together, these observations suggest that constraint level may influence how each learner balances familiar strategies, teacher modeling, and exploratory problem-solving. 

## Emerging Questions

* How do task novelty, reward novelty, and environmental novelty differently affect motivation, regulation, and cognitive output?
* When do familiar visual patterns support task initiation, and when might they limit adoption of a more efficient strategy?
* How does constraint level affect learner-specific problem-solving approaches in spatial tasks?
* How does teacher modeling influence strategy adoption when learners already have a familiar entry point?
